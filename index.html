Types of Email Fraud

Understanding the different attack vectors is the first step to effective defense. Here are the most prevalent email-based fraud schemes active today.

Phishing

Mass emails impersonating legitimate organizations to steal credentials, payment info, or install malware through deceptive links.

Critical 36% of all breaches

BEC / CEO Fraud

Business Email Compromise where attackers impersonate executives to authorize fraudulent wire transfers or sensitive data theft.

Critical $2.7B losses in 2024

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks using personal information gathered from social media to craft convincing, personalized fraudulent emails.

High AI-enhanced targeting

Romance Scams

Fraudsters build fake relationships via email and dating platforms, eventually manipulating victims into sending money or financial info.

High $1.3B reported losses

Delivery Scams

Fake shipping notification emails claiming package delivery issues, prompting victims to click malicious links or pay bogus fees.

Medium Surge during holidays

MFA Bypass

Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM) phishing proxies that intercept authentication sessions and bypass multi-factor authentication.

Critical Emerging threat 2024

Defense Strategies

How to Protect Yourself

Actionable steps individuals and organizations can take to prevent email fraud and minimize damage from attacks.

For Individuals

1

Verify Before You Click

Hover over links to preview URLs. Never click links in unexpected emails — instead, navigate to the website directly by typing the address.

2

Check the Sender's Address

Look carefully at the full email address, not just the display name. Scammers use lookalike domains (e.g., @bankofameriica.com with extra 'i').

3

Enable MFA Everywhere

Use hardware security keys (YubiKey) or authenticator apps. Avoid SMS-based MFA which is vulnerable to SIM swapping attacks.

4

Never Send Sensitive Data via Email

Legitimate organizations won't ask for passwords, SSNs, or banking details via email. When in doubt, call the organization directly.

5

Watch for Urgency & Pressure

Phishing emails create false urgency ("Your account will be suspended in 24 hours"). Take a breath and verify through official channels.

For Organizations

1

Implement DMARC, DKIM & SPF

Deploy email authentication protocols to prevent domain spoofing. Set DMARC policy to "reject" for maximum protection against impersonation.

2

Conduct Regular Phishing Simulations

Run monthly simulated phishing tests to measure employee susceptibility and provide targeted training. Track click rates and improve over time.

3

Enforce Out-of-Band Verification for Payments

Require phone or in-person verification for any wire transfer or payment change request. This single measure prevents most BEC losses.

4

Deploy AI-Powered Email Security

Use next-gen email security platforms with AI/ML detection that can identify BEC patterns, anomaly-based threats, and zero-day phishing campaigns.

5

Have an Incident Response Plan

Prepare a documented IR plan specific to email compromise. Include immediate containment steps, forensic procedures, and FBI IC3 reporting contacts.

Red Flags

10 Signs of a Phishing Email

🚨

Urgent Language

"Act now!" "Immediate action required" — creating panic to bypass critical thinking.

🔗

Mismatched URLs

Link text says one thing, actual URL goes somewhere completely different.

✏️

Grammar Errors

Spelling mistakes, awkward phrasing, and inconsistent formatting. (Less common with AI now.)

👤

Generic Greeting

"Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name. Though AI can personalize now too.

📎

Suspicious Attachments

Unexpected .exe, .zip, .docm, or .xlsm files. Especially from unknown senders.

🎁

Too Good to Be True

Prize winnings, lottery notifications, inheritance claims, or unbelievable discounts.

🏢

Spoofed Domain

Lookalike domains with extra characters, hyphens, or wrong TLDs (e.g., .xyz, .top).

🔒

Requests for Credentials

Any email asking you to "verify" or "confirm" your password, SSN, or banking info.

⏰

Odd Timing

Emails arriving at unusual hours, or referencing events/transactions you don't recognize.

🎭

Pressure to Bypass Process

Requests to bypass normal procedures, skip approvals, or keep transactions "confidential."

AI has transformed phishing in several ways: (1) LLMs generate perfect, grammatically flawless emails in any language, eliminating the grammar/spelling red flags. (2) AI can scrape social media to craft hyper-personalized spear-phishing that references real events, relationships, and context. (3) Voice deepfakes enable vishing attacks that perfectly clone executives' voices for BEC. (4) AI-generated images and video deepfakes add convincing visual elements to scams. (5) Phishing-as-a-service platforms use AI to automate campaign creation at massive scale. The barrier to entry for sophisticated attacks has dropped dramatically.

Phishing is mass, untargeted — sent to thousands or millions of people, usually impersonating well-known brands. Spear phishing is targeted at specific individuals or organizations, using gathered intelligence to appear legitimate. Whaling is a subset of spear phishing that targets high-value individuals — CEOs, CFOs, board members — typically for BEC or executive fraud. The level of personalization and research increases from phishing → spear phishing → whaling, as does the potential financial impact per successful attack.

Yes, Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM) phishing can bypass most MFA. Here's how: The attacker creates a proxy server that sits between the victim and the legitimate login page. When the victim enters credentials on the fake page, the proxy forwards them to the real service in real-time. When the real service sends an MFA prompt, the proxy passes it through to the victim. The victim approves what looks like a normal login, but the proxy now has a valid session cookie that it stole during the exchange. This bypasses SMS, authenticator app, and even push notification MFA. Defense: FIDO2 hardware security keys (like YubiKey) with origin binding are immune to AiTM because the key verifies the actual domain, not the proxy.
